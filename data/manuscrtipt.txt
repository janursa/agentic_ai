Abstract
Aging profoundly impair immune competence, increasing infection risk and weakening vaccine efficacy, yet the regulatory mechanisms underlying this decline and their reversibility remain unclear. Here, we analyzed over 25 million single-cell transcriptomes from over 2000 individuals aged 20-90 years, spanning six cohorts and diverse ancestries to construct the Human Immune aging Regulation Atlas (HIaRA), a comprehensive map of transcriptional and regulatory remodeling in aging, disease, and interventions. We identified over 600 transcription factors with age-associated changes in inferred activities, with CD8⁺ T subsets exhibiting the strongest regulatory rewiring, characterized by increased effector differentiation and interferon signaling and loss of naïve programs . Using data from 1,200 individuals, we developed fast, transcriptionally interpretable, cell type-specific aging clocks that accurately predicted biological age and revealed accelerated T-cell aging in systemic lupus erythematosus, most pronounced in younger patients. Screening 90 cytokine and 146 drug perturbations uncovered IL-10 and ruxolitinib, JAK-STAT modulators, as potent rejuvenating agents. Complementary ex vivo experiments verified that ruxolitinib counteracts age- and infection-associated regulatory signatures, partially restoring youthful transcriptional programs. Together, these results define the regulatory architecture of immune aging and demonstrate its pharmacological reversibility, providing a framework for interventions to maintain immune resilience.
Introduction
Aging is a fundamental biological process and a major driver of morbidity and mortality worldwide, underlying an increased risk of cancer, cardiovascular disease, and neurodegeneration1–3. Among the biological systems most affected is the immune system, which undergoes a progressive functional decline known as immunosenescence 4,5. Age-related immune deterioration manifests as increased vulnerability to infections, impaired vaccine responsiveness, and persistent low-grade inflammation 6,7,  collectively contributing to frailty in older adults.

At the molecular level, immune aging involves both shifts in cell composition and coordinated transcriptional, epigenetic and proteomic changes 8,9 10,11 that alter cellular identity and function. Central to these changes are gene-regulatory networks (GRNs), which integrate transcription factor (TF) state, chromatin accessibility and signaling cues to maintain immune homeostasis. With age, GRN integrity deteriorates through altered TF activity and chromatin remodeling, leading to dysregulated gene expression and impaired immune responsiveness 12. Understanding immune aging as a systems-level drift in regulatory network dynamics offers a mechanistic framework for identifying drivers of immune decline and potential targets for intervention.

Previous studies have described age-associated transcriptional changes in immune cells 13–16, but most have been limited by small sample sizes, lack of demographic diversity, or narrow cell-type coverage. Consequently, the field lacks a comprehensive map of transcriptional regulation in immune aging. In parallel, aging clocks derived from molecular data have emerged as promising tools for quantifying biological age and identifying interventions that modulate aging trajectories   9,17–24. However, most transcriptomic clocks are derived from global gene expression profiles and therefore primarily capture downstream consequences of regulatory drift rather than its upstream drivers. Integrating gene regulatory networks with aging clocks has the potential to provide mechanistic insight into the transcriptional regulation that shapes immune aging.

Here, we present the Human Immune aging Regulation Atlas (HIaRA), a single-cell resource comprising more than 25 million immune cells from over 2000 individuals aged 20 to 90 years across six cohorts of multiple ancestries (Fig. 1). Using this dataset, we define the transcriptional regulatory landscape of immune aging, identifying over 600 TFs with age-associated activity changes, with T cells showing the strongest network rewiring. We develop GRN-integrated, cell type–specific aging clocks that predict biological age with high accuracy and provide enhanced transcriptional interpretability. Through systematic screening with aging clocks and transcriptional mapping of age-associated regulatory signatures, we reveal accelerated immune aging in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) and identify JAK-STAT modulators of IL-10 and ruxolitinib as potent rejuvenating agents that counteract both physiological and infection-induced immune aging trajectories. Together, HIaRA provides a comprehensive regulatory framework for understanding immune aging, offers avenues for targeted intervention, and is made interactively available through an online portal .

Age-related trajectories of gene regulation across immune cell types
To delineate how gene-regulatory programs change with age in the human immune system, we analyzed more than 18 million single-cell transcriptomes from 5 independent cohorts spanning different ancestries and balanced for sex and age (Methods, Fig. 1, Supplementary Fig. 1A). The combined dataset comprised approximately 2000 donors aged 20–90 years, with peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) profiled and processed through a harmonized pipeline. We used four cohorts of OneK1K, ABF300, AIDA, and Perez for discovery and the SoundLife cohort for validation.
We inferred cell type–specific GRNs from these single-cell datasets and quantified TF activity for each donor, providing a functional readout of regulatory influence (Methods). For each immune cell type, TF activity was modeled as a function of chronological age within individual cohorts. We conducted meta-analysis across discovery cohorts to identify robust, directionally consistent age-associated TFs. TFs were considered significantly associated with age if they exhibited a concordant change (increase or decrease) across the discovery cohorts, a meta-analyzed false discovery rate (FDR) below 0.05, and a Spearman correlation of over 0.1 across all cohorts (Methods). 
Using discovery cohorts, we identified more than 600 TFs across different immune lineages, exhibiting significant age-associated changes in inferred transcriptional activity (Fig. 2A). Both gains and losses in TF activity were observed, indicating that immune aging involves bidirectional remodeling rather than a uniform decline. The most extensive rewiring occurred in T cells, with approximately 350 and 120 age-associated TFs detected in CD8+ and CD4+ subsets (Fig. 2A–B). This pervasive rewiring aligns with the central role of T cells in immunosenescence, characterized by contraction of naïve populations, expansion of effector memory and terminally differentiated effector populations (TEMRA) subsets, and reduced clonal diversity 25,26. Age-associated TFs were enriched in inflammatory and effector pathways such as TNF-α signaling via NF-κB, IL-2/STAT5, and Interferon Gamma response, indicating heightened immune activation with age (Methods, Supplementary Fig. 2A). In contrast, activity of TFs associated with Wnt–β-catenin signaling declined, consistent with reduced maintenance of naïve T cells, a hallmark of immunosenescence. 
The validation analyses showed strong concordance with the discovery results (Supplementary Fig. 2B), with more than 95 percent of age-associated TFs exhibiting consistent directionality (increased or decreased with aging) across all lineages. Nonetheless, over 20 percent of age-associated TFs did not reach statistical significance in the validation cohort (Supplementary Fig. 2C, Supplementary Table 1 ). These included members of the IRF family (IRF2, IRF4, and IRF9) in CD8⁺ T-cell subsets, known regulators of effector–memory fate decisions 27,28, which displayed strong age associations in the discovery analysis (FDR < 1e−32) but failed to reach significance in validation set despite preserving the same aging trajectories. This highlights the importance of our large sample sizes for detecting subtle aging effects, where the discovery analysis leveraged approximately 1,800 donors, whereas the validation set comprised only ~100 donors. Consistent with this interpretation, the small subset of directionally discordant TFs (Supplementary Fig. 2C, Supplementary Table 2 ) was entirely restricted to those with weak age correlations in the validation cohort (Spearman |ρ| < 0.2), further suggesting limited statistical power rather than true biological disagreement.
Age-associated TFs tended to occupy highly central positions within their respective GRNs (Methods, Fig. 2C), indicating that aging affects fundamental cellular programs. Among the most important altered TFs, PRDM1, TBX21, and KLF6 exhibited increased activity with age across T and NK cells, whereas LEF1, TCF7, and BACH2 decreased. These patterns mirror known functional trajectories: PRDM1 and TBX21 promote effector differentiation 13 whereas BACH2 and LEF1 sustain naïve and regulatory programs 29. A more comprehensive comparative analysis revealed considerable overlap of aging-associated TFs between CD4+, CD8+ T cells, and NK cells (Supplementary Fig. 2D), consistent with their shared lineage and common transcriptional programs underlying T-cell activation and effector responses. Ten transcription factors were shared across all three cell types, several of which exhibited conserved age-associated trends (for example, a decline in SATB1; Fig. 2D and E), while others displayed lineage-specific divergence (for example, GATA3 upregulated in T cells but downregulated in NK cells; Fig. 2D and F). This cross-lineage comparison provides the first comprehensive view of regulatory programs that shape immune aging across the lymphoid compartments.
Because inferred TF activity integrates the coordinated expression of target genes, it can diverge from TF mRNA abundance. While some TFs (e.g., SATB1 and GATA3) showed concordant changes in both activity and expression, others (NME2), a critical TF for T-cell activation and calcium signaling 30,31, displayed marked age-associated shifts in activity without detectable changes in expression (Supplementary Fig. 2E), underscoring the added value of TF activity analysis beyond simple expression profiling.
Collectively, these analyses delineate the regulatory architecture of immune aging, revealing lineage-specific and shared transcriptional programs that underpin functional decline. A complete catalogue of age-associated TFs and their target modules is available via our interactive HIaRA web portal.

GRN-informed cell type-specific aging clocks 
To quantify immune aging at the level of transcriptional regulation, we developed cell type–specific aging clocks informed by GRNs (Methods). For each immune cell type, we selected predicted GRN target genes as model features (Supplementary Fig. 3A), rather than the entire transcriptome as in previous approaches 9,21. This method captures biologically meaningful features while reducing noise and computational costs. Moreover, it yields interpretable models in which each feature can be traced to upstream TF regulators. 
Clocks were trained on pseudobulk transcriptomes from approximately 1,200 healthy donors aged 18–90 years and validated across three independent cohorts with over 800 donors, spanning multiple ancestries. Among several regression algorithms tested, Ridge regression provided optimal accuracy and generalizability. Direct single-cell models performed less robustly (results are not shown) and were computationally intensive; therefore, pseudobulk models were used for subsequent analyses. 
The GRN-informed clocks accurately predicted chronological age, achieving Spearman correlations of approximately 0.8 for both CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells (Fig. 3A). Performance was lower in NK cells, monocytes, and B cells, consistent with previously observed heterogeneity and weaker age-associated signatures in these cell types (Fig. 1). Previous aging clocks were also less successful in these cell types compared to CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells 9. Across the test datasets, GRN-informed clocks outperformed previous models trained on genome-wide gene expression 9 (Supplementary Fig. 3B). Considering that both clocks were trained on the same datasets, such an improvement could be attributed to biologically constrained feature selection and/or increased robustness due to pseudobulking (we did not explicitly experiment with individual factors). More notably, our clocks are computationally efficient, requiring seconds per sample, and are distributed as an open-source Python package. 
Pathway enrichment analysis of the genes selected by the GRNs for aging clocks revealed an overrepresentation of immune-related processes across cell types, including interferon-α and interferon-γ responses, TNF-α signaling via NF-κB, and apoptosis (Fig. 3B). These findings are consistent with our previous observations of enriched pathways among age-associated TFs (Supplementary Fig. 2A) and align with pathways recurrently implicated in immune aging 32–34.
Inspection of top-weighted features confirmed biological coherence: genes with positive weights increased in expression with age, whereas negatively weighted genes declined (Fig. 3C and Supplementary Fig. 3C). Many top-ranked features were established markers of immune aging and senescence, including CD70 35, CDKN2A/p16INK4a 36–38, and KLRC1/NKG2A 39,40, which have been previously implicated in T-cell exhaustion, senescence-associated phenotypes, and age-related immune dysfunction.
Next, to dissect the regulatory programs underlying these clocks, we integrated GRNs with gene associations to the clocks and identified TFs with the highest regulatory link to aging (Methods). In CD8+ T cells, the TFs exerting the strongest regulatory influence were JUN, KLF6, GATA3, MAF, and SOX4, while in CD4+ T cells, the top regulators were SOX4, IRF4, RORC, SCML4, and SATB1 (Fig. 3C and Supplementary Fig. 3C). All of these TFs have been previously implicated in immune aging or organismal senescence, both in our analyses (Supplementary Fig. 3D) and in prior studies 14,29,41–45, underscoring their conserved roles in shaping age-related transcriptional programs across T-cell lineages. A notable exception was RORC, a master regulator of Th17 cell differentiation, which showed increased activity with age but a negative regulatory coefficient in the CD4+ T cell clock, meaning that increased activity of this TF would reduce the predicted biological aging. Since aging clocks capture multifactorial and conditional relationships between regulatory programs, whereas our TF activity analysis evaluates marginal age associations, this discrepancy likely reflects the context-dependent role of RORC within correlated aging pathways, potentially acting as a compensatory or counter-regulatory signal in CD4⁺ T cells rather than a direct driver of aging. This example highlights the importance of GRN-integrated aging clocks, identifying TFs with different roles within complex multi-regulatory environments versus their isolated age trajectories.
Together, these results establish interpretable, cell type–specific aging clocks that integrate transcriptional and regulatory information to quantify biological aging in the human immune system.
Autoimmune activation accelerates T-cell aging in young individuals with systemic lupus erythematosus
To investigate how disease-associated immune activation resembles aging trajectories, we applied our aging clocks to SLE, a prototypical autoimmune disorder characterized by chronic inflammation and premature immune dysfunction. We analyzed transcriptomes from 250 individuals with healthy controls and SLE patients 46, with balanced group sizes, predominantly female donors, and ages ranging from 20 to 80 years (Supplementary Fig. 1A).
Both CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells from SLE patients showed significantly elevated predicted biological ages compared to age-matched controls (Methods; Fig. 3D). This acceleration was most pronounced in younger patients for CD8T (age<50 years, +6 years, p = 1e⁻11), but not in older individuals (Supplementary Fig. 3E), paralleling clinical patterns in which immune hyperactivation dominates early disease and exhaustion and comorbidities prevails later 47. These results suggest that SLE accelerates transcriptional aging, particularly in early adulthood.
To dissect underlying mechanisms, we identified TFs with significantly altered activity in SLE across all immune cell types (Supplementary Fig. 2F). SLE induced widespread transcriptional perturbations across all cell types, exhibiting both shared and cell type–specific patterns. CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells showed the highest concordance, consistent with their close lineage relationship. Gene set enrichment analysis revealed upregulation of proinflammatory and effector pathways, including IL-2/STAT5, interferon-α and interferon-γ responses, and TNF-α signaling via NF-κB, coupled with downregulation of Wnt–β-catenin signaling (Supplementary Fig. 3G). These pathways mirrored those associated with physiological aging in CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cells (Supplementary Fig. 2A), supporting the view that autoimmunity accelerates aging programs. 
Comparison of TF activity profiles between SLE and healthy individuals demonstrated that nearly all age-associated TFs shifted concordantly in SLE (Fig. 3E). Among the 15 most central age-associated TFs, SLE reproduced the same directional activity shifts observed with advancing age (Fig. 3F and Supplementary Fig. 3F). For example, LEF1, a regulator of naïve T-cell maintenance 48,49, showed premature downregulation, activity in 30-year-old SLE patients resembled that in 50-year-old healthy controls. Together, these results indicate that autoimmune activation re-engages core regulatory programs of immune aging and that such acceleration is most evident in younger adults with SLE in CD8T.

Systematic cytokine perturbation identifies IL-10 as a rejuvenating regulator of T cell aging
Having established that chronic immune activation accelerates transcriptional aging, we next sought to identify interventions capable of reversing these molecular trajectories. We applied our cell type-specific aging clocks to a public cytokine perturbation dataset 50, which includes transcriptomic profiles from PBMCs treated with 90 different cytokines, each tested in 12 donors and profiled 24 hours post-treatment (Methods). For each perturbation, we estimated the biological age and compared the treated versus untreated samples to quantify age-modifying effects (Methods). 
Our analysis revealed distinct functional classes of cytokines based on their effects on biological age (Fig. 4A and Supplementary Fig. 3G and H). Age-accelerating cytokines were predominantly pro-inflammatory mediators including members of the interleukin family such as IL-2, IL-4, IL-7, and IL-15. Conversely, age-rejuvenating cytokines comprised primarily anti-inflammatory and immunoregulatory factors, including IL-10, IL-22, IL-6, and OSM. Interestingly, type I interferons (IFN-β, IFN-ω) exhibited opposing effects between CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, accelerating aging in CD4+ T cells while promoting rejuvenation in CD8+ T cells, suggesting cell-type-specific responses to interferon signaling in the context of biological aging. 
IL-10, a key anti-inflammatory mediator acting through JAK1/TYK2-mediated STAT3 signaling 51,52, emerged as the most potent age-rejuvenating factor across both cell types, reducing predicted biological age by 4.9 years in CD4+ T cells (p < 0.0001) and 2.0 years in CD8+ T cells (p < 0.0001), highlighting its potential as a therapeutic target for counteracting immunological aging. Probing its regulatory basis, IL-10 treatment showed significant shift in TF activity across all lineages (Supplementary Fig. 2F). IL-10 suppressed multiple immune-related pathways, including IL-2/STAT5 signaling and interferon-α and interferon-γ responses across both cell types (Supplementary Fig. 2G). These shifts were directionally opposite to those observed in aging and SLE (Supplementary Fig. 2A and G), suggesting restoration of youthful transcriptional states. IL-10 restored the activity of the majority of age-associated TFs (Fig. 4B and Supplementary Fig. 2H). For example, in CD8⁺ T cells, IL-10 restored the activity of ~300 out of ~370 age-associated TFs. This reversal was especially pronounced among highly central TFs within the aging GRN, including LEF1 and TCF7, key regulators of naïve and memory T-cell programs whose activities decline with age. Together, these results align with aging-clock predictions and indicate that IL-10 remodels T-cell regulatory networks toward a younger configuration.
IL-10 has been extensively studied for its anti-disease effects 53–55 and anti-aging properties 56,57. In T cells specifically, IL-10 has been shown to restore function to terminally exhausted CD8+ T cells by metabolically reprogramming them toward oxidative phosphorylation, thereby enhancing their proliferation and effector capabilities 58. Furthermore, IL-10 has been shown to preserve mitochondrial integrity and function in T cells exposed to suppressive tumor microenvironments, enabling sustained anti-tumor responses 59. Here, we show for the first time that IL-10 directly counteracts immune aging at the transcriptional regulatory level by reversing age-associated TF activity programs and restoring youthful gene regulatory network states in human T cells. 
Pharmacological reversal of immune-aging signatures
Next, we applied our cell type-specific aging clocks to a public drug perturbation dataset 60,61, which includes transcriptomic profiles from PBMCs treated with 146 small molecules, each tested in triplicate donors and profiled 24 hours post-treatment (Methods). The compounds spanned a broad range of influences on predicted age. Some agents, such as CGM-097, an MDM2 inhibitor that activates p53 and has been linked to senescence 24, increased predicted biological age in both CD4⁺ T and CD8⁺ T cells (Supplementary Fig. 3I). This aligns with their known pro-senescent actions in immune cells 62. In contrast, 16 compounds in CD4⁺ T and one compound in CD8⁺ T significantly reduced predicted age (p < 0.05; Supplementary Fig. 3J) (compound names withheld). Among these candidates, we disclose the JAK1/2 inhibitor ruxolitinib, an approved therapy for myelofibrosis and polycythemia vera, which exhibited significance in rejuvenating CD4⁺ T cells among our identified compounds (p = 0.023; Fig. 4C). Ruxolitinib suppresses JAK/STAT signaling 63 and has been shown to attenuate cellular senescence and ameliorate age-related phenotypes across diverse experimental models 64–66. Transcriptional analysis revealed that ruxolitinib broadly counteracted age-associated regulatory programs, reversing the activity of the majority of aging-associated TFs (Fig. 4D). Ruxolitinib suppressed multiple immune-related pathways, including TNF-α signaling via NF-κB, IL-2/STAT5 signaling, interferon-α and interferon-γ responses in CD4+ cells (Supplementary Fig. 2G). These shifts were directionally opposite to those observed in aging and SLE and mirrored the effects of IL-10 treatment (Supplementary Fig. 2A and G). Comparative transcriptional analysis of ruxolitinib and IL-10 perturbations further demonstrated highly concordant effects, reflected by consistent alterations in TF activity profiles (Supplementary Fig. 2K). Notably, key JAK-STAT pathway transcription factors including STAT1, STAT2, STAT3, IRF1, IRF2, IRF7, IRF9, and NFKB2 67–71 showed consistent directional changes in response to both treatments.
We performed an independent ex vivo experiment to further analyze the rejuvenating effects of ruxolitinib, in which PBMCs from seven healthy donors were treated with ruxolitinib for 18 hours and compared against two baseline conditions of RPMI (negative control) and lipopolysaccharide (LPS) (Methods). We previously demonstrated that infections such as SARS-CoV-2 induce cell type–specific age acceleration within the immune system 9. We therefore asked whether ruxolitinib could reverse the inflammaging potentially induced by LPS in addition to the baseline conditions. Indeed, LPS stimulation induced a pronounced acceleration of immune aging as quantified by aging clock (p = 1e-16; Fig. 4C), consistent with our previous findings that SARS-CoV-2 infection induces age acceleration 9. Ruxolitinib treatment significantly reduced predicted age under baseline conditions (p = 0.048) and showed a partial, though not statistically significant, rejuvenation following LPS stimulation (p = 0.12).
Transcriptional analysis of the validation cohort confirmed our previous observations that ruxolitinib counteracts aging-associated transcriptional patterns (Supplementary Fig. 2J). This effect was most pronounced among the top 15 central aging TFs, where LPS stimulation induced aging-like signatures, whereas ruxolitinib consistently reversed these changes (Fig. 4E). For example, STAT1 and BATF—whose activities increase with aging—were significantly upregulated by LPS stimulation, while ruxolitinib treatment significantly reduced their activity. STAT1 and BATF are key regulators of interferon signaling and T-cell differentiation, respectively, whose sustained activation has been linked to chronic inflammatory states 51,52 and T-cell exhaustion during persistent immune activation 72,73. Thus, their suppression by ruxolitinib is consistent with a shift toward a less inflammatory and more functionally competent CD4⁺ T-cell state. These data suggest that ruxolitinib can counteract both chronological and inflammation-driven immune aging at the transcriptional-regulatory level.
These findings, aligned with those previously observed for IL-10, support a role for JAK–STAT signaling in modulating age-associated immune regulatory programs.

Discussion 
This study presents the Human Immune aging Regulation Atlas (HIaRA), a comprehensive single-cell resource that defines the transcriptional regulatory architecture of human immune aging. By integrating over 25 million immune transcriptomes from over 2000 individuals, we identified more than 600 TFs with age-associated changes in regulatory activity, constructed transcriptionally interpretable aging clocks, and demonstrated that immune aging can be quantitatively modeled and modulated through interventions. The study yields four main advances. First, it maps a global landscape of regulatory drift across immune cell types, revealing that T cells—particularly CD8⁺ subsets—undergo the most extensive network rewiring. Second, it establishes fast and accurate GRN-integrated aging clocks implemented as an open-source computational toolkit. Third, it uncovers disease-dependent acceleration of immune aging in SLE, most evident in younger patients. Finally, it identifies JAK-STAT modulars of IL-10 and ruxolitinib as potent agents that reverse age-associated transcriptional signatures in both healthy and infectious environments. HIaRA is made publicly available as an interactive web platform to facilitate exploration of age-, disease-, and intervention-associated regulatory changes across immune lineages.
The SLE analyses provided molecular evidence that autoimmune activation recapitulates key features of physiological aging. The near-complete overlap between SLE- and age-associated TF signatures suggests that regulatory programs underlying immune decline in aging can be pathologically re-engaged in inflammatory disease. This convergence reinforces the concept that aging presents a modifiable, rather than fixed, biological state. The observation that disease-related age acceleration was most pronounced in younger individuals implies that immunological youth may confer greater susceptibility to pathological aging signals, whereas older individuals, whose immune systems are already extensively remodeled, exhibit smaller additional shifts. Future work should test whether this pattern extends to other autoimmune and inflammatory conditions.
Our findings also point to pharmacological avenues for rejuvenating immune function. Among 90 cytokines and 146 drugs screened, IL-10 emerged as the strongest cytokine candidate and ruxolitinib as a modest drug candidate for reversing transcriptional aging, reducing predicted biological age and restoring youthful TF-activity profiles. The convergence between aging, SLE activation, and IL-10/ruxolitinib reversal supports a central role for JAK–STAT signaling in immune aging. Despite engaging different nodes of the JAK–STAT pathway, IL-10 signaling and ruxolitinib produce highly concordant effects on age-associated TF activity, suggesting convergence on shared downstream regulatory programs. However, the precise magnitude of identified rejuvenating effects remains uncertain: for example, reductions in predicted age ranged from 2 to 5 years for IL-10 across different T cell lineages to 2 to 10 years for ruxolitinib across discovery and validation analyses. These discrepancies likely reflect technical variation across cohorts and the inherent limitations of transcriptomic clocks compared to epigenetic benchmarks. Future work integrating multi-omics modalities may refine these estimates and better quantify the therapeutic potential of JAK modulation. Moreover, the current analysis focused on ex vivo experiments; longitudinal in vivo and clinical studies are needed to determine the durability, systemic relevance, and safety of pharmacological interventions that target immune aging. Nonetheless, several other candidate compounds in our screen also showed significant age-reversal potential, whereas others accelerated predicted aging. Both categories warrant further mechanistic and translational investigation.
Despite its scope, this study has several limitations. First, the association of TF activity with age modeled as a monotonic relationship; non-linear trajectories and inflection points across the lifespan 74 are likely to exist and could be captured using more flexible statistical frameworks. Second, our cohorts primarily included donors of European and Asian ancestry, potentially limiting generalizability to other populations. Third, sex-specific differences in immune aging were not assessed, despite well-documented sexual dimorphism in immune responses 75,76. Fourth, our analysis focused on major immune cell types; finer subpopulation resolution, particularly within B cells and myeloid lineages, may reveal additional regulatory programs, as previous studies have shown that B cells are notably affected by aging 77–79. Fifth, HIaRA relies on transcriptomic data alone. Integrating chromatin accessibility, proteomic, and epigenomic information will provide a more complete view of regulatory aging. Future analyses could also infer age-resolved or subpopulation-specific GRNs to capture structural shifts in regulatory connectivity over time. Finally, we evaluated SLE as a disease case study and analyzed two perturbation datasets of cytokines and drugs as a proof of principle, demonstrating that our integrated framework combining aging clocks with transcriptional mapping can be broadly applied to immune-mediated and age-related disorders, as well as to the evaluation of therapeutic interventions.
In summary, HIaRA provides a unified framework for dissecting the regulatory underpinnings of immune aging, quantifying biological age at the level of transcriptional control, and identifying candidate interventions to restore immune resilience. By linking single-cell regulatory dynamics to therapeutic screening, this work establishes that the molecular hallmarks of immune aging are not fixed but can be rewired by targeted pharmacological modulation. The atlas and accompanying analytical tools lay a foundation for mechanistically informed interventions aimed at delaying or reversing age-associated immune decline across the human lifespan.

Methods
Curation of public datasets
Here, we describe the preprocessing of individual public datasets used in this study. Figure 1 and Supplementary Figure 1A summarize the key characteristics of each dataset, including the total number of single cells, the number of donors, and the distribution of samples across age, sex, and ethnicity. All reported statistics were computed after applying the quality control procedures described in the following section.
OneK1K cohort
The OneK1K cohort 80 was accessed via CELLxGENE . It includes PBMCs from 981 European individuals aged over 17 years at recruitment. Only healthy, unstimulated individuals were included in this analysis. PBMCs were processed using the 10x Genomics Single Cell 3′ Library and Gel Bead Kit and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 2000 platform. CellRanger (v2.2.0) was used to generate the gene expression matrix. 
 ABF300 cohort
The ABF300 cohort 81 consists of PBMCs from 166 healthy, non-obese, and non-smoking Caucasian individuals aged 25–85 years. This cross-sectional and short-term longitudinal study was conducted between 2018 and 2021. A total of 317 samples were processed using 10x Genomics 3′ single-cell RNA-seq with feature barcoding, along with TCR and BCR sequencing. Libraries were sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq S4. Data were processed with Cell Ranger (v7.0.0) and Seurat (v4.0.5), with demultiplexing performed using both hashtag- and genotype-based approaches. The dataset is available from the Synapse repository (accession: syn49637038).
Zhang cohort
This cohort was obtained from the Synapse repository (accession: syn61609846) corresponding to the immune-aging atlas published by Zhang et al 82. This study profiled PBMCs across the entire human lifespan. For our analysis, we included 33 participants aged 20 to 90 years with available scRNA-seq data. PBMCs were processed using the 10x Genomics 5′ single-cell RNA-seq protocol with paired TCR/BCR profiling and sequenced on Illumina platforms. The processed Seurat object (scRNA-seqProcessedLabelledObject.rds) was downloaded, and only healthy donors were retained for analysis.
AIDA cohort
The AIDA Freeze v1 83, comprises approximately 1.26 million circulating immune cells profiled from 619 healthy donors across seven population groups in five Asian countries. Library preparation was performed using the 10x Genomics 5′ scRNA-seq protocol, and raw read processing and demultiplexing were performed centrally with the Illumina DRAGEN Single-Cell RNA pipeline.
Perez SLE cohort
This cohort, corresponding to the study by Perez et al 46, was accessed via CELLxGENE (https://cellxgene.cziscience.com/collections/436154da-bcf1-4130-9c8b-120ff9a888f2). It includes PBMCs from 162 SLE cases and 99 healthy controls profiled by multiplexed single-cell RNA sequencing (mux-seq). Libraries were prepared using the 10x Genomics Chromium platform and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq. Cell Ranger (v3.1) was used for initial data processing. The processed h5ad file was downloaded, Ensembl gene identifiers were converted to gene symbols, and raw counts were extracted.
SoundLife cohort
The SoundLife cohort was obtained from 84. It contains approximately 12M single-cell RNA data of PBMCs from 96 donors collected longitudinally, sequenced using the 10x Genomics Chromium 3′ v3 chemistry. The cohort comprises healthy young adults (25–35 years; N = 47) and older adults (55–65 years; N = 45), with balanced sex distribution. Participants were recruited from the greater Seattle, WA area and screened to exclude individuals with a history of chronic or autoimmune diseases, chronic infections, or severe allergies. The dataset includes both cytomegalovirus-positive and -negative individuals, longitudinal measurements at multiple time points following influenza vaccination, as well as baseline samples. The data were provided with prior quality control and cell-type annotations.
ParseBioscience cohort
This cohort, sourced from 85, contains approximately 10M single-cell RNA-seq profiles of PBMCs from 12 donors (6 male and 6 female). Cells were exposed to 90 cytokine perturbations for 24 hours, with PBS-treated samples serving as controls. Sequencing data were processed using the ParseBiosciences Analysis Pipeline v1.4.0, and the resulting data were provided with quality control and cell-type annotations applied. Since the publicly provided dataset did not include age information for the donors, we obtained this information through direct communication with the authors.
OPSCA dataset 
We sourced this data from 86, which originates from the 2023 Open Problems: Single Cell Perturbation competition. It consists of single-cell drug perturbation data on PBMCs, where the cells were distributed across 96-well plates, with two columns reserved for positive controls (Dabrafenib and Belinostat) and one column for the negative control compound dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO). The positive controls were chosen for their well-characterized transcriptional effects. The remaining wells on each plate were assigned to 144 distinct compounds, one per well. Experiments were performed using cells from three donors, resulting in a total of six plates (two per donor). Samples from each row were pooled prior to sequencing, and read processing was performed by the study organizers using the Cell Ranger pipeline. 
Generation of drug perturbation dataset
A complementary drug perturbation dataset was generated from PBMCs of seven healthy donors. PBMCs were isolated using SepMate tubes (STEMCELL Technologies) and resuspended in RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (Biowest). In a total volume of 200 μL per well, 0.5 × 10⁶ PBMCs were cultured in round-bottom 96-well plates (SARSTEDT) and stimulated ex vivo with 1 μM ruxolitinib (InvivoGen) plus 10 ng/mL lipopolysaccharide (LPS; Sigma) or maintained in RPMI medium as control for 18 h at 37 °C and 5% CO₂. Single-cell suspensions were processed using the Chromium X instrument and the Chromium GEM-X Single Cell 3′ Reagent Kits (v4, 10x Genomics) to generate single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq) libraries. Libraries were sequenced on Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform. The resulting dataset underwent the same quality control, cell type annotation, and normalization procedures as described above.
Data preprocessing
Quality control was performed at both the gene and cell levels. We applied the following thresholds: minimum 100 genes per cell, maximum 5,000 genes per cell 87, and minimum 10 cells per gene per donor. Cell type annotations were performed consistently across datasets using CellTypist 88. The coarse model classified cells into five major immune populations: CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, NK cells, B cells, and monocytes. For the datasets of SoundLife and ParseBioscience, we used the already available cell labels. For pseudobulk analysis, raw single-cell counts were aggregated by summing counts across cells within each cell type, donor, time (if multiple measurements per donor), and well (if given). We excluded bulked samples with fewer than 10 single cells 89. Data were normalized using a shifted logarithm transformation: sequencing depth was corrected with the Scanpy normalize_total function, followed by a log1p transformation to stabilize variance 87. 
Inference of transcription factor activity
To estimate TF activity scores, we first inferred GRNs from single-cell data of discovery cohorts. Only genes observed in at least 500 single cells were retained to ensure sufficient statistical power.  GRN inference was performed by computing pairwise Spearman correlations between all genes within each cell type and cohort, followed by restricting the set of putative regulators to a curated list of known TFs 86, retaining only TF–gene pairs with significant correlations (p < 0.05), and selecting the top 100,000 edges per cell type and condition. 
We benchmarked this correlation-based GRN inference strategy against state-of-the-art GRN inference methods using the geneRNIB evaluation framework 86. Despite its simplicity, the Spearman correlation approach demonstrated competitive performance across all evaluation metrics while remaining substantially more computationally efficient (Supplementary Note). This computationally efficient approach enabled us to infer GRNs from single-cell data, as single-cell data has shown superior biological relevance in GRN inference compared to pseudobulked alternatives 86. 
The inferred GRN models for each cell type and cohort showed varying degrees of overlap in TF–gene pairs across cohorts (Supplementary Fig. 1B). To harmonize these models, we constructed a consensus GRN for each cell type by retaining TF–gene edges present in at least two cohorts and exhibiting a consistent direction of effect (regulatory weight) across all datasets. The consensus networks contained 60,000–90,000 edges, with the highest overlap observed among CD8T, CD4T, and NK cells, consistent with their shared lineage (Supplementary Fig. 1C). These cell type–specific consensus GRNs were used for all subsequent analyses.
TF activity scores were then inferred by computing the dot product between each GRN adjacency matrix and the corresponding gene expression matrix (Supplementary Fig. 1D), using Decoupler univariate linear model 90. We used pseudobulked expression data to estimate TF activity, which has been shown to yield more stable results than single-cell level calculations 90. 
Identification of transcription factors associated with aging, disease, and drug perturbation
To identify TFs associated with aging using discovery cohorts, we computed Spearman correlations between TF activity scores and pseudobulked samples for each cohort and cell type. Of note, for Perez SLE cohort, we only considered the healthy samples for this analysis. This produced correlation coefficients (range: –1 to 1) along with p-values for each TF, cell type, and dataset. We then performed a meta-analysis across cohorts using Fisher’s method 91 to combine evidence. Multiple testing correction was applied to the combined p-values, and TFs with an adjusted meta p-value < 0.05, a consistent slope, and a correlation above 0.1 across cohorts were considered significantly associated with aging. For validation of age-associated TFs in the SoundLife cohort, we correlated TF activity with age for each TF and cell type, and considered associations significant if FDR < 0.05 and correlation > 0.1.
To identify TFs associated with SLE, we compared SLE patients with healthy individuals in the Perez cohort. Specifically, we applied a Mann–Whitney U test 92 to assess differences in TF activity between healthy and disease samples for each TF and cell type, followed by multiple testing correction within each cell type. For the cytokines and drug perturbation datasets (discovery and validation), we used a mixed-effects model to identify TFs with significantly with altered activities, with perturbation included as a fixed effect and donor as a random effect. Multiple testing correction was applied separately for each cell type, and TFs with adjusted p-values < 0.05 were considered significant. 
Gene set enrichment analysis
Gene set enrichment analysis was performed using cell type–specific sets of significant TFs associated with aging, SLE, and ruxolitinib. For each cell type and condition, TFs with increased or decreased activity were analyzed separately. Only TFs with adjusted p-values below 0.05 were included in the analysis. Enrichment was conducted using the MSigDB Hallmark 2020 gene set collection as the reference database, implemented via the GSEApy package 93. 
Construction of GRN-integrated aging clocks 
We built cell type–specific aging clocks using genes derived from GRN models as predictor features. The number of genes used per clock ranged from approximately 4,000 to 5,000 depending on the cell type, with roughly 2,000 genes shared across cell types and varying degrees of overlap among other genes (Supplementary Fig. 3A). Models were trained on gene expression data from 1,200 donors across the OneK1K and ABF300 cohorts to predict chronological age. 
We tested multiple regression approaches, including Ridge regression, Gradient Boosting, Elastic Net, and multilayer perceptrons (MLPs). Ridge regression, tuned via leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation, consistently achieved the best performance in both cross-validation predictions and held-out test cohorts (results not shown). We also experimented with training models directly on single-cell data by aggregating median expression values per donor. However, models trained on pseudobulked data outperformed these single-cell based models (results not shown). Predictive performance was evaluated using the Spearman correlation between predicted and actual chronological age.
Calculation of transcriptional regulators of aging clocks
Because the aging clocks were built using target genes from GRN models, we quantified the regulatory contribution of TFs to each clock’s predictions. After training the models, we obtained the regression coefficients of the target genes, which are quantitative values where magnitude reflects the strength of the effect and the sign indicates the direction—for example, a positive coefficient means that higher gene expression leads to an increased predicted age. We then estimated the contribution of individual TFs to the aging clock output using:
A_m= W_(m×n)  × w_n
where Am represents the inferred contribution of each TF (m) to the clock, W_(m×n) is the TF–target gene weight matrix derived from the GRN, and w_n corresponds to the learned gene weights from the trained aging clock. This approach allows direct mapping of the model’s predictive features to upstream regulatory factors, providing a transcriptional interpretation of the clocks. Aₘ contains continuous positive and negative values; for example, a positive value indicates that increased activity of the TF is associated with a higher predicted age.
Age acceleration and rejuvenation analysis using aging clocks
 We used aging clocks to estimate age acceleration or rejuvenation under given conditions. For the disease analysis in the SLE cohort, predicted age was compared between donors with and without reported SLE. Statistical significance was assessed using a Wilcoxon rank-sum test followed by FDR correction. Age acceleration was defined by a significant adjusted p-value (< 0.05) and an increased slope. This analysis was performed once for all age groups and once separately for young and old age groups to examine the effect of chronological age on SLE-associated age acceleration, using two thresholds (40 and 50 years) to increase robustness. For the cytokine and drug perturbation datasets (discovery and validation), we applied a mixed-effects model with perturbation as a fixed effect and donor as a random effect. Multiple testing correction was applied, and an adjusted p-value <0.05 was used to determine significance. Rejuvenation was defined as a significant decrease in predicted age relative to control, whereas acceleration was defined as a significant increase in predicted age.

Supplementary note
Benchmark of GRN inference 
In this study, we inferred GRNs using pairwise Spearman correlations to quantify gene–gene associations. We subsequently filtered these associations by restricting regulators to a curated list of known TFs (Methods). This simple correlation-based approach offers substantial computational efficiency compared to more sophisticated GRN inference methods, although it may conflate correlation with causation. In the absence of true ground truth, direct validation of causal regulatory relationships remains infeasible. Instead, GRN inference methods are typically assessed using benchmarking frameworks that combine diverse datasets with multiple evaluation metrics.

Here, we employed the geneRNIB framework 86, which leverages the largest available perturbation datasets for GRN inference and evaluation, to benchmark our approach against more advanced methods from the literature. Specifically, we performed GRN inference and evaluation using Spearman correlation on five PBMC-derived datasets provided by geneRNIB and compared the results against twelve integrated GRN inference methods: GRNBoost2, SCENIC+, FigR, GRaNIE, CellOracle, scGLUE, SCENIC, PORTIA, PPCOR, scGPT, GeneFormer, and scPRINT. The first five methods are multi-omics GRN inference approaches that leverage paired scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq data, whereas the remaining seven methods rely solely. Overall, the Spearman correlation approach demonstrated competitive performance and outperformed most alternative methods across datasets and evaluation metrics (Supplementary note Fig. 1). These findings are consistent with previous benchmarking studies reporting that Pearson correlation outperformed more complex GRN inference algorithms 86.

Among the evaluated methods, only GRNBoost2 and SCENIC+ showed superior performance relative to Spearman correlation. However, these gains come at a substantial computational cost: GRNBoost2 requires roughly two orders of magnitude more computation than Spearman correlation, rendering it impractical for large-scale analyses. For instance, inferring a GRN from 20,000 single cells and 10,000 genes takes approximately 10 hours with GRNBoost2, whereas the Spearman correlation approach completes the same task in minutes. Scaling GRNBoost2 to datasets comprising millions of single cells would therefore be computationally prohibitive.
